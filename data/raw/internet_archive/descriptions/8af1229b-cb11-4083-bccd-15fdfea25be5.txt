Common Sense was a highly polemical, semi-monthly tabloid that became a centerpiece of far-right political discourse in the mid-20th-century United States. It was established in 1946 by Conde McGinley in Union, New Jersey, Common and was published weekly in the United States by Douglas T. Kaye until 1972. The publication positioned itself as ""The Nation's Anti-Communist Paper,"" but its scope extended far beyond conventional Cold War anxieties. It frequently alleged that the U.S. government and the United Nations were being subverted by ""invisible" Marxist forces. It was also notorious for disseminating antisemitic tropes, often framing global political shifts as part of a "Zionist conspiracy." It was frequently cited by the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) and the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) for its extremist content. The tabloid staunchly opposed the Civil Rights Movement, characterizing it as a communist-led initiative designed to destabilize American social structures.
