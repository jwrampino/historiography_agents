The Gentleman's Magazine was a British general-interest periodical published monthly between 1731 and 1922. Founded by English printer, editor and publisher Edward Cave (1691–1754), it was the first to use the term ‘magazine’—coined by Cave and taken from the Italian magazzino or French magasin in reference to a storehouse—and published news and commentary on a range of topics. Though the magazine ceased general publication in 1907, issues consisting of four pages each were printed in very small editions until 1922 in order to keep the title formally "in print". Edited under the pseudonym “Sylvanus Urban”, each issue featured editorials and articles on a variety of subjects, including antiquarian studies, history, natural sciences, art, politics, literature, poetry, theater and medicine. An image of St John’s Gate in Clerkenwell, London (the magazine’s original office and printing house) appeared on the title page of every issue from its inauguration. During its tenure, the magazine published contributions from a number of distinguished writers, including John Hawkins, Charles Woodmason, Edward Young, Arthur Murphy, Jonathan Swift, Elizabeth Carter (under a nom de plume ), James Thomson, Richard Savage, Peregrine Branwhite, Anna Eliza Bray, William Roscoe, Samuel Bowden, Mark Akenside, Samuel Boyse, Thomas Christie, and William Falconer, among others.&nbsp; Indeed, the&nbsp; English writer Samuel Johnson (1709–1784), often called Dr Johnson, began writing for Cave regularly as a young poet. Through Johnson’s introduction, the actor and playwright David Garrick (1717–1779) also wrote a series of pieces for inclusion in the magazine, and performed Henry Fielding’s (1707–1754) comedy The Mock Doctor in an upstairs room at St John’s Gate in 1740, with himself in the title role of “Gregory” who poses as a doctor, and the magazine’s staff reading the remaining parts. Along with poetry and politics, Cave also utilized the magazine to promote his own interest in science. A close follower of contemporary developments in the study of electricity, he was particularly interested in the experiments of the American polymath, Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790). Inspired by Franklin’s descriptions of a metal rod placed atop a building to draw lightning from the clouds, Cave erected his own on top of St John’s Gate.
